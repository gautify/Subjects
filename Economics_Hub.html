The bedrock: what economics is, why it exists, and its two grand branches — Micro and Macro.

What is Economics? Foundational

Economics is the study of how individuals, firms, and societies allocate scarce resources to satisfy unlimited wants. The core problem is simple: resources are finite, but desires are not.

The word comes from the Greek oikonomia — "management of a household." In modern terms, it's about decision-making under constraints.

🥜
Scarcity
Resources — land, labour, capital — are limited. Choices must be made about how to use them.
⚖️
Trade-off
Choosing one option means giving up another. Every decision involves a trade-off.
💸
Opportunity Cost
The value of the next best alternative you give up. The true cost of any choice.
🇮🇳 Indian Example
A farmer in Punjab has 1 acre of land. He can grow wheat or rice. If he chooses wheat and earns ₹50,000, the opportunity cost is the profit foregone from rice (say ₹40,000). The real cost of growing wheat is not just seeds and labour — it's the ₹40,000 he didn't earn from rice.
💡 FYI
Lionel Robbins (1932) gave the most famous definition: "Economics is the science which studies human behaviour as a relationship between ends and scarce means which have alternative uses." This definition shifted economics from studying wealth to studying choices.
Positive vs Normative Economics Foundational
TypeWhat it saysExample
Positive Factual statements — what is. Can be tested empirically. "A 10% rise in petrol prices leads to a 6% fall in petrol consumption."
Normative Value judgments — what ought to be. Cannot be tested. "The government should subsidise electric vehicles for the poor."
💡 FYI
Most policy debates mix positive and normative. For example: "Petrol tax will reduce emissions" (positive) + "we should reduce emissions" (normative) = "we should raise petrol tax." Good economists label which part is which.
Economic Models & Ceteris Paribus Core

Economic model: A simplified representation of reality, like a map. It highlights what matters and ignores the rest. Examples: demand-supply model, IS-LM, AS-AD.

Ceteris Paribus (Latin): "All other things being equal." When economists say "a price rise reduces demand, ceteris paribus," they mean — holding income, preferences, prices of related goods constant.

🇮🇳 Indian Example
When the RBI (Reserve Bank of India) raises the repo rate, the model predicts borrowing will fall — ceteris paribus. But in practice, if simultaneously the government announces a big infrastructure push, investment may not fall. Models are tools, not perfect predictors.
Production Possibility Frontier (PPF) Foundational

The PPF shows all combinations of two goods an economy can produce when all resources are fully and efficiently used. It illustrates scarcity, trade-offs, opportunity cost, and efficiency simultaneously.

Production Possibility Frontier
  • Points on PPF (A, B): Productively efficient — all resources used
  • Point inside PPF (D): Inefficient — unemployment, idle resources
  • Point outside PPF (C): Currently unattainable — needs more resources or better technology
  • Outward shift of PPF: Economic growth — better technology, more resources, education
  • Concavity: Resources are not perfectly adaptable → increasing opportunity cost as you specialise more
🇮🇳 India's PPF Shift
India's PLI (Production Linked Incentive) scheme for semiconductors and mobile manufacturing aims to shift India's PPF outward — by boosting productive capacity. Similarly, investment in education and health (National Education Policy, Ayushman Bharat) improves human capital, shifting PPF upward over the long run.
Core Vocabulary Foundational
Factors of Production
Inputs used to produce goods and services: Land (natural resources), Labour (human effort), Capital (machinery, tools), Enterprise (entrepreneurship). Together: LLCE.
Utility
Satisfaction or usefulness a consumer gets from a good/service. A subjective measure — measured in "utils" theoretically.
Equilibrium
A state where there is no tendency to change. In a market: when quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
Elasticity
The responsiveness of one variable to a change in another. E.g., how much does quantity demanded change when price changes?
Market
Any arrangement where buyers and sellers interact to exchange goods, services, or resources. Need not be a physical place.
Marginal
The additional change from one more unit. "Marginal cost" = cost of producing one more unit. Thinking at the margin is central to economics.
Rational Agent
Classical assumption: individuals make decisions to maximise their utility/profit, given constraints. Real people often deviate (see Behavioural Economics).
Market Failure
When free markets lead to inefficient outcomes — e.g., pollution, public goods, monopoly, information asymmetry.
GDP
Gross Domestic Product — total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a year.
Inflation
Sustained rise in the general price level. Reduces the purchasing power of money. Measured by CPI, WPI, or GDP Deflator in India.
Fiscal Policy
Government decisions on spending (G) and taxation (T) to influence the economy. Expansionary: ↑G or ↓T. Contractionary: ↓G or ↑T.
Monetary Policy
Central bank (RBI in India) decisions on money supply and interest rates to control inflation and growth. Tools: repo rate, CRR, SLR, OMOs.
Economic Agents Foundational

Economics involves three main decision-makers:

👤
Households (Consumers)
Supply labour & other factors; demand goods and services. Goal: maximise utility given a budget constraint.
🏭
Firms (Producers)
Hire factors of production; supply goods and services. Goal: maximise profit.
🏛️
Government
Regulates markets, provides public goods, redistributes income, runs fiscal and monetary policy.
Microeconomics vs Macroeconomics Foundational

Economics is broadly divided into two branches. They study different scales of the same economy.

MicroeconomicsMacroeconomics
Focus Individual agents — consumers, firms, markets Economy as a whole — national output, inflation, employment
Key questions Why does the price of onions rise? How does a consumer decide what to buy? Why does India's GDP grow at 7%? What causes inflation?
Unit of analysis One market, one firm, one consumer Entire national or global economy
Key models Demand-Supply, Indifference Curves, Cost curves IS-LM, AD-AS, Phillips Curve, Solow Growth Model

💡 FYI — The "Fallacy of Composition"
What's good for one agent isn't always good for the whole economy. If one firm cuts wages to reduce costs, it's rational. But if all firms do it, aggregate demand falls — everyone's worse off. This is why micro logic doesn't always translate to macro. Keynes made this a central point.
🇮🇳 Real Connection
When onion prices rose sharply in India (micro event), it contributed to headline CPI inflation (macro measure). The government's response — banning onion exports — was a micro intervention with macro consequences (currency effects, trade balance impact).

Microeconomic Theory — The Big Picture Core

Microeconomics is built on a single powerful idea: rational agents maximise their objectives subject to constraints.

  • Consumers maximise utility subject to a budget constraint
  • Firms maximise profit subject to cost constraints
  • Markets coordinate these decisions through prices

When these decisions interact in a market, they produce an equilibrium price and quantity. Understanding why this equilibrium forms — and breaks down — is the core of micro.

👤
Consumer Theory
How do people choose what to buy? Utility, indifference curves, budget constraints, revealed preferences.
📉
Demand & Supply
Market equilibrium. How prices and quantities are determined. The engine of market economics.
🏭
Producer Theory
Costs of production, profit maximisation, supply decisions. (See What You Missed for market structures.)
💡 FYI — The Price Mechanism
Adam Smith called it the "invisible hand." Prices coordinate millions of individual decisions — no central planner needed. When tomatoes are scarce, prices rise; farmers grow more; shortage resolves. This self-correcting mechanism is the marvel of market economics.
Utility & The Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility Core

Utility (U): The satisfaction or benefit a consumer receives from consuming a good or service. Measured in hypothetical units called "utils."

Total Utility (TU): Total satisfaction from consuming a given quantity of a good.

Marginal Utility (MU): The additional utility gained from consuming one more unit.

Formula
MU = ΔTU / ΔQ

Where: ΔTU = change in Total Utility, ΔQ = change in quantity (usually = 1)

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility (DMU): As a consumer consumes more of a good, the additional (marginal) utility from each successive unit decreases, ceteris paribus.

🇮🇳 Example — Chai
Cups of ChaiTotal Utility (utils)Marginal Utility (utils)
1st cup3030
2nd cup5020
3rd cup6010
4th cup622
5th cup60−2 (disutility!)
TU rises but at a decreasing rate. MU eventually becomes negative — you've had too much chai!
💡 Why it matters
DMU explains why demand curves slope downward. As you consume more, each unit is worth less to you → you're only willing to pay less for each additional unit → hence lower price = higher quantity demanded.
Consumer Equilibrium — Equimarginal Principle Core

A consumer with a fixed budget allocates spending across goods to maximise total utility. The optimal condition is:

Consumer Equilibrium Condition
MUₓ / Pₓ = MUᵧ / Pᵧ = ... = MUₙ / Pₙ

Marginal utility per rupee spent must be equal across all goods.
Also written as: MUₓ / MUᵧ = Pₓ / Pᵧ
🇮🇳 Example
Priya has ₹100. She can buy tea (₹10/cup) or samosas (₹20/piece). If MU of tea = 20 utils and MU of samosa = 30 utils, then: MU/P for tea = 20/10 = 2 utils/₹, MU/P for samosa = 30/20 = 1.5 utils/₹. Tea gives more utility per rupee → she should buy more tea until MU/P equalises.
Indifference Curves & MRS Advanced

An Indifference Curve (IC) shows all combinations of two goods that give the consumer the same level of utility. The consumer is "indifferent" between these bundles.

Properties of Indifference Curves:

  • Downward sloping: More of one good requires less of the other to maintain same utility
  • Convex to origin: Reflects DMU — as you consume more of good X, you're willing to give up less Y for one more X
  • Non-intersecting: Two ICs cannot cross (would violate transitivity)
  • Higher IC = higher utility: Curves farther from origin represent more of both goods
Indifference Curves & Budget Constraint
■ Higher IC (IC₂) = more utility  |  ■ Budget constraint  |  ● Optimal choice (tangency point E*)

MRS (Marginal Rate of Substitution): The rate at which a consumer is willing to give up one good in exchange for another, keeping utility constant.

MRS Formula
MRS = −ΔY / ΔX = MUₓ / MUᵧ

MRS diminishes as we move along the IC (convexity assumption)

Budget Constraint: Shows all combinations a consumer can afford given income (M) and prices.

Budget Constraint
Pₓ · X + Pᵧ · Y = M
Slope = −Pₓ / Pᵧ (negative price ratio)
X-intercept = M/Pₓ, Y-intercept = M/Pᵧ

Consumer Optimum (tangency condition):

Optimality Condition
MRS = Pₓ / Pᵧ
At optimum: slope of IC = slope of budget constraint
🔮 Deep-dive — Corner Solutions
When a consumer buys only one good (e.g., only rice, no vegetables), the optimum is at a corner of the budget line — MRS ≠ price ratio. This happens when goods are not substitutes (perfect complements) or the budget is too tight.
Law of Demand & Demand Curve Foundational

Demand: The quantity of a good that consumers are willing and able to buy at each price, during a given time period, ceteris paribus.

Law of Demand: There is an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded — as price rises, Qd falls, and vice versa.

Reasons for this: (1) Substitution effect — as price rises, relatively cheaper substitutes are preferred. (2) Income effect — higher price reduces real purchasing power. (3) DMU — at higher consumption, extra unit is worth less.

Demand & Supply — Market Equilibrium
■ Demand (D) — slopes down  |  ■ Supply (S) — slopes up  |  ● Equilibrium (E*)

Shifts in Demand (the whole curve moves — not just along it):

  • ↑ Income (for normal goods) → demand shifts right (↑D)
  • Rise in price of substitute → demand shifts right
  • Fall in price of complement → demand shifts right
  • ↑ Population, ↑ Positive expectations → demand shifts right
  • Change in tastes/preferences
🇮🇳 Onion Example, 2023
Floods in Maharashtra and Karnataka destroyed onion crops → supply fell sharply → supply curve shifted left → with same demand, prices shot to ₹100–200/kg. This is a shift in supply, not a movement along it.
Law of Supply & Equilibrium Foundational

Law of Supply: There is a positive relationship between price and quantity supplied — as price rises, Qs increases.

Reason: Higher price = higher profit margin = more incentive to produce.

Shifts in Supply:

  • ↓ Cost of production (cheaper raw materials) → supply shifts right
  • Better technology → supply shifts right
  • Government subsidy → supply shifts right
  • Favourable weather (agriculture) → supply shifts right

Equilibrium: Where Qd = Qs. The market "clears" — no excess demand or supply.

  • Excess Demand (shortage): Qd > Qs → price rises → Qd falls, Qs rises → equilibrium restored
  • Excess Supply (surplus): Qs > Qd → price falls → Qs falls, Qd rises → equilibrium restored
💡 Price Ceiling & Floor
Price Ceiling (max price, like rent control): set below equilibrium → creates excess demand (shortage). E.g., India's essential commodities price caps.
Price Floor (min price, like MSP): set above equilibrium → creates excess supply. E.g., India's Minimum Support Price for wheat ensures farmers sell at a minimum price — but can lead to government surplus stock.
Substitution Effect & Income Effect Core

When the price of a good changes, two effects operate simultaneously:

Substitution Effect
When the price of good X rises, it becomes relatively more expensive than substitutes. Consumers substitute away from X → demand for X falls. Always negative (opposite direction to price change).
Income Effect
When price of X rises, real purchasing power falls — as if income fell. For normal goods: demand falls further. For inferior goods: demand may rise (real income fall → buy more inferior good).
Good TypeSubstitution EffectIncome EffectNet Effect on Demand
Normal goodNegative (↓Qd)Negative (↓Qd)Both reinforce — Demand curve slopes ↓
Inferior goodNegative (↓Qd)Positive (↑Qd)SE > IE → Demand curve slopes ↓
Giffen goodNegative (↓Qd)Positive (↑Qd)IE > SE → Demand curve slopes ↑ (!)
💡 Giffen Goods
Named after Sir Robert Giffen who observed that when bread prices rose in 19th century England, poor people bought more bread — because they could no longer afford meat. In India, this is observed with coarse grains like jowar/bajra among very poor households. Giffen goods are theoretically fascinating but empirically rare.
Consumer Surplus & Willingness to Pay Core

Willingness to Pay (WTP): The maximum price a consumer would voluntarily pay for a good. Reflects their subjective valuation.

Consumer Surplus (CS): The difference between what a consumer is willing to pay and what they actually pay.

Consumer Surplus
CS = WTP − Actual Price Paid

In a market: CS = Area of triangle above price line & below demand curve
CS = ½ × Base × Height = ½ × Q* × (P_max − P*)
Consumer Surplus (shaded region)
Blue triangle = Consumer Surplus at market price P*
🇮🇳 Example — Airline Tickets
Rahul is willing to pay ₹8,000 for a Mumbai-Delhi flight. IndiGo charges ₹5,500. His consumer surplus = ₹2,500. Anita was willing to pay only ₹5,000 → she doesn't buy. Price discrimination (business vs economy class) is airlines' way of capturing consumer surplus.
Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) Core

PED measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.

Price Elasticity of Demand
PED = % Change in Qd / % Change in P
PED = (ΔQd/Qd) / (ΔP/P)

PED is always negative (law of demand), but we usually report its absolute value.
Type|PED| valueMeaningExample
Perfectly inelastic0Qd doesn't change at allInsulin, emergency medicines
Inelastic0 to 1Qd changes less than proportionally to pricePetrol, salt, onions (short run)

Perfectly elastic∞Any price rise → zero demandPerfect competition (theoretical)

Total Revenue (TR) Test:

Total Revenue Effect
TR = P × Q

Elastic demand: Price ↑ → TR ↓ (quantity falls more than price rises) Inelastic demand: Price ↑ → TR ↑ (quantity falls less than price rises) Unit elastic: Price ↑ → TR unchanged
🇮🇳 Example — Petrol Tax
India's petrol demand is inelastic in the short run (people need to commute). So when the government raised excise duty on petrol, revenue went up — despite some fall in consumption. In the long run, demand becomes more elastic as people switch to CNG, EVs, public transport.
💡 Determinants of PED
1. Availability of substitutes: More substitutes → more elastic (e.g., Pepsi vs. cola). Few substitutes → inelastic (medicines).
2. Necessity vs. luxury: Necessities are inelastic; luxuries elastic.
3. Time period: Demand is more elastic in the long run (time to adjust).
4. Proportion of income: Higher share of income → more elastic (e.g., car vs. newspaper).
Income Elasticity (YED) & Cross-Price Elasticity (XED) Core
Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)
YED = % Change in Qd / % Change in Income

YED > 0 → Normal good (demand rises with income) YED > 1 → Luxury good (demand rises faster than income) YED < 0 → Inferior good (demand falls as income rises) 0 < YED < 1 → Necessity (demand rises slowly with income)
🇮🇳 Indian Context — Engel's Law
As incomes rise in India, the share of expenditure on food falls (though absolute spending rises) — Engel's Law. Demand for motor vehicles (YED > 1) has surged as India's middle class grows. Meanwhile, coarse grain (jowar, bajra) demand falls — classic inferior goods.
Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand (XED)
XED(A,B) = % Change in Qd of A / % Change in Price of B

XED > 0 → A and B are substitutes (e.g., tea and coffee) XED < 0 → A and B are complements (e.g., cars and petrol) XED = 0 → Unrelated goods
🇮🇳 Example — Substitutes & Complements
Tea and coffee in India are substitutes — if Tata Tea prices rise, demand for Nescafé rises (XED > 0). Cars and petrol are complements — if petrol prices spike, demand for large SUVs falls (XED < 0). This is why Maruti Suzuki watches crude oil prices closely.

Durable Goods & Elasticity Advanced

Durable goods (refrigerators, cars, TVs) have high elasticity because:

  • Purchases can be postponed — consumers wait for price drops
  • Used market exists as a substitute
  • They represent large shares of income

This creates a policy challenge: taxing durable goods can sharply reduce their demand and hurt producers disproportionately.

💡 FYI — Dorfman-Steiner Theorem
Firms with inelastic demand should spend more on advertising. A luxury brand like Louis Vuitton can charge high prices (low PED) but must advertise heavily to maintain the image that keeps demand inelastic.
Externalities Core

An externality occurs when a market transaction creates costs or benefits for third parties — people not involved in the transaction. Externalities cause market failure — the market price doesn't reflect true social costs/benefits.

Negative Externality
Third-party cost. Social Cost > Private Cost → Market over-produces. Example: Factory pollution in the Ganga. MSC > MPC → output too high.
Positive Externality
Third-party benefit. Social Benefit > Private Benefit → Market under-produces. Example: Education — benefits not just the student but society. MSB > MPB → output too low.
Externality Concepts
MSC = MPC + Marginal External Cost (negative externality) MSB = MPB + Marginal External Benefit (positive externality)
Social Optimum: MSC = MSB Market Outcome: MPC = MPB (ignores externality)

Pigouvian Tax/Subsidy: Government can "internalise" externalities by taxing negative externalities (carbon tax) or subsidising positive ones (education grants).

🇮🇳 Examples
Negative: Delhi's vehicular pollution. The social cost of driving (smog, health damage) far exceeds the private cost (fuel + car maintenance). Odd-even scheme, BS-VI norms try to reduce this.

Positive: India's vaccination programmes. A vaccinated person protects others too (herd immunity). Without government subsidies, vaccination would be under-provided.
Network Effects: Bandwagon & Snob Effects Advanced

Standard demand theory assumes consumers' choices are independent. In reality, they often aren't:

Bandwagon Effect
Demand increases because others are also buying. ("Everyone has it, so I want it.") Makes demand more elastic to price rises. Example: WhatsApp, Jio SIM cards, UPI payments.
Snob Effect
Demand decreases as more people buy. ("I want it because it's exclusive.") Makes demand less responsive to price cuts. Example: Luxury watches, rare artwork, first-edition books.
💡 Veblen Goods
Goods for which demand increases as price rises — because high price signals status. Named after Thorstein Veblen. Example: Rolex, high-end diamond jewellery. The demand curve can slope upward for these goods.
Say's Law & Paradox of Public Policy Advanced

Say's Law: "Supply creates its own demand." Jean-Baptiste Say (1803) argued that producing goods automatically generates income to buy them — the economy naturally tends to full employment. This is the classical view.

Keynes challenged this: demand can fall short of supply → recession. "Supply does NOT always create its own demand."

💡 Paradox of Thrift (Keynes)
If one person saves more, they're prudent. But if everyone saves more simultaneously, aggregate demand falls, firms earn less, they cut wages, and savings actually fall in aggregate. What's rational individually is harmful collectively — a classic macro paradox.

Paradox of Public Policy: Government intervention, though well-intentioned, can create unintended consequences:

  • Rent Control: Intended to protect tenants → landlords don't invest → housing stock deteriorates → housing shortage worsens
  • Minimum Wage: Intended to help workers → some firms reduce hiring → unemployment can rise
  • MSP in India: Intended to protect farmers → creates excess supply → FCI accumulates grain it can't distribute efficiently
Cost Concepts — Fixed, Variable & Total Core

A firm's costs determine how much it supplies and at what price. Understanding cost structure is central to supply-side economics.

Cost Formulas
TC = TFC + TVC (Total Cost = Fixed + Variable) MC = ΔTC / ΔQ (Marginal Cost = change in TC per unit) ATC = TC / Q (Average Total Cost) AFC = TFC / Q (Average Fixed Cost — always falls) AVC = TVC / Q (Average Variable Cost) ATC = AFC + AVC
Short-Run Cost Curves — U-shaped ATC & MC
■ MC  |  ■ AVC  |  ■ ATC  |  ╌ AFC (always falling)

Key Rules:

  • MC always passes through the minimum point of both AVC and ATC
  • When MC < ATC → ATC is falling. When MC > ATC → ATC is rising
  • AFC always falls (fixed costs spread over more units)
  • Short run: at least one input fixed (usually capital). Long run: all inputs variable
🇮🇳 Example — Small Steel Plant
A steel plant in Jharkhand has a blast furnace (fixed cost: ₹100 crore/yr regardless of output). Labour and raw material are variable. As output rises, AFC falls sharply. Initially, specialisation raises efficiency (MC falls); but eventually congestion and bottlenecks raise MC again — classic U-shaped curve.
Profit Maximisation — MR = MC Rule Core

A firm maximises profit where Marginal Revenue = Marginal Cost. At this output, the extra revenue from one more unit just equals the extra cost — no more gain from producing further.

Profit Maximisation
π = TR − TC (Profit = Total Revenue − Total Cost) MR = dTR/dQ = P (for perfect competition, since P is constant) MC = dTC/dQ Optimal: MR = MC

Shut-down condition (short run): P < AVC → firm shuts down (can't cover variable costs) Break-even: P = ATC → zero economic profit (normal profit only) Supernormal profit: P > ATC → TR > TC

💡 Economic Profit vs Accounting Profit
Accounting profit = Revenue − Explicit (cash) costs.
Economic profit = Revenue − Explicit costs − Implicit costs (opportunity costs like owner's foregone salary, own capital).
In long-run perfect competition, economic profit → 0. Firms earn only "normal profit" — enough to keep them in the industry, but no excess.
Returns to Scale & Economies of Scale Advanced
Returns to Scale (Long Run)
If inputs increase by λ and output increases by: > λ → Increasing Returns to Scale (IRS) — economies of scale = λ → Constant Returns to Scale (CRS) < λ → Decreasing Returns to Scale (DRS) — diseconomies of scale

Economies of Scale: Long-run ATC falls as output rises. Reasons: specialisation, bulk buying, spreading fixed costs, technology.

Diseconomies of Scale: Long-run ATC rises beyond some output. Reasons: management complexity, coordination failures, communication breakdowns in large organisations.

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES): The lowest output at which long-run ATC is minimised. Industries with high MES tend toward oligopoly or monopoly (natural monopoly if MES covers entire market demand).

🇮🇳 Indian Context
Reliance Jio achieved massive economies of scale — ₹1.5 lakh crore network built once, then amortised across 450+ million subscribers. Per-user cost falls dramatically. This created a natural entry barrier: newcomers can't replicate the cost structure. TRAI had to balance competition vs. network investment incentives.

Business Cycle & Recession Basics Core

The business cycle refers to the fluctuations in economic activity (GDP, employment, inflation) over time. It has recurring phases:

Business Cycle Phases
PhaseWhat happensIndian Indicator
ExpansionGDP grows, employment rises, investment picks upIIP (Index of Industrial Production) rises, credit growth high
PeakHighest point of economic activity, inflation often highRBI begins raising rates; Sensex near highs

TroughLowest point; economy at most depressed stateFY2020-21 India: −6.6% GDP growth (COVID)

Causes of Recession:

  • Demand shock: Sharp fall in consumer confidence or spending (COVID lockdowns)
  • Supply shock: Oil price spike, war disruption (1973 OPEC crisis)
  • Financial crisis: Bank failures, credit crunch (2008 global financial crisis)
  • Policy error: Excessive tightening by central bank (US 2022–23 rate hikes)
  • External shock: Export demand collapse, global slowdown
💡 India's COVID Recession (FY21)
India's GDP contracted by 6.6% in FY2020-21 — worst since independence. The cause: massive demand shock (lockdowns) + supply disruption. The recovery (V-shaped) was driven by pent-up demand, vaccination, and government stimulus (PMGKP, ₹20 lakh crore package).

Macroeconomics — The Big Picture Core

Macroeconomics was born from the Great Depression (1929–33) — when classical economics failed to explain why markets didn't self-correct. John Maynard Keynes provided the framework.

The central questions of macro:

  • What determines a country's output and income? (GDP)
  • What causes prices to rise? (Inflation)
  • Why are people out of work? (Unemployment)
  • How do economies grow over time? (Growth)
  • How do policy tools affect the economy? (Fiscal & Monetary Policy)
💡 Three Schools of Thought
Classical/Neoclassical: Markets self-correct; prices flexible; long-run focus; government shouldn't interfere.
Keynesian: Markets can fail; prices sticky; short-run focus; government can stabilise through demand management.
Monetarist: Money supply is the key driver; government spending ineffective long-run; Friedman's "k% rule."
GDP — Gross Domestic Product Foundational

GDP is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period (usually a year or quarter).

Key words: final (not intermediate), within borders (not by citizens), monetary value (current market prices).

Expenditure Approach — Most Common
GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)

C = Private Consumption (household spending) I = Gross Investment (business investment + residential construction + inventory) G = Government Expenditure (not transfer payments) X = Exports M = Imports (X − M) = Net Exports (Trade Balance)
🇮🇳 India's GDP Composition (approx.)
C ≈ 57% | I ≈ 32% | G ≈ 11% | Net Exports ≈ −2% (India runs a trade deficit). India's GDP at current prices (FY24): ~₹295 lakh crore (~$3.5 trillion). 5th largest economy globally.
Other Approaches
Income Approach: GDP = Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit + Mixed Income
Value-Added Approach: GDP = Sum of Net Value Added across all sectors
NVA = Gross Output − Intermediate Consumption

Wealth vs Income:

💡 What GDP Misses
GDP doesn't capture: (1) Non-market goods — household work, volunteering, subsistence farming. (2) Distribution — India's GDP/capita may rise but inequality could worsen. (3) Environment — GDP rises when forests are felled and sold as timber. (4) Happiness & wellbeing. That's why HDI, GPI (Genuine Progress Indicator), and Green GDP are proposed alternatives.
Circular Flow of Income Core

The economy is a circular flow between two sectors — households and firms — through two types of markets.

Circular Flow of Income
━━ Money flows  |  ╌╌ Real flows (goods/services)  |  ━━ Income payments

In a closed economy: Total Output = Total Income = Total Expenditure. These three approaches to GDP always give the same result.

Measuring Inflation Core

Inflation: A sustained, general rise in the price level. Reduces the purchasing power of money. Not to be confused with a price rise in one good (that's a relative price change).

CPI — Consumer Price Index
Measures price changes of a fixed basket of goods consumed by a typical household. India's headline inflation measure. Base year: 2012. Compiled by MOSPI.
WPI — Wholesale Price Index
Measures prices at the wholesale (factory-gate) level. Covers about 697 commodities. Base year: 2011-12. Often leads CPI changes. Compiled by DPIIT.
GDP Deflator
Broader measure — covers all goods in GDP, not just a fixed basket. Updates automatically to include new goods. Best for measuring overall price level change.
PPI — Producer Price Index
Measures prices received by producers. Similar to WPI. India is transitioning to PPI to align with global standards.
CPI Calculation
CPI = (Cost of basket in current year / Cost of basket in base year) × 100

Inflation Rate = ((CPI₂ − CPI₁) / CPI₁) × 100
Or approximately: % change in CPI
🧮 CPI Inflation Calculator

Types of Inflation:

  • Demand-pull: Too much money chasing too few goods. "Inflation by demand." (Post-COVID India, 2021-22)
  • Cost-push: Supply shock raises production costs → firms raise prices. (Russia-Ukraine war → wheat/oil price surge 2022)
  • Built-in / Wage-price spiral: Workers demand higher wages → firms raise prices → workers demand higher wages...
  • Imported inflation: Depreciation of rupee makes imports costlier → price rise (India's oil import bill)
💡 RBI's Inflation Target
Under the Monetary Policy Framework (2016), RBI targets CPI inflation at 4% ± 2% (2%–6% band). If inflation remains outside this band for 3 consecutive quarters, RBI must explain to the government. This is the Flexible Inflation Targeting (FIT) framework.

Three Pillars of Keynesian Macro Core

Keynes' General Theory (1936) built macroeconomics on three foundational relationships:

🛒
Consumption (C)
C = a + b·Yd. Households consume a baseline + a fraction (MPC) of disposable income. MPC (Marginal Propensity to Consume) is key.
🔧
Investment (I)
I = I₀ − b·r. Investment is negatively related to the interest rate (r). Lower rates → cheaper to borrow → more investment. Highly volatile (animal spirits).
🏛️
Government (G & T)
G = autonomous government spending. T = taxes. Both shift the demand schedule. Fiscal policy works through these levers.
Keynesian Consumption Function & Multiplier
C = a + MPC · Yd (a = autonomous consumption)
MPC + MPS = 1

Multiplier (k) = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPS
ΔGDP = k × ΔG (or k × ΔI)

If MPC = 0.8 → k = 1/(1-0.8) = 5 So a ₹100 crore increase in government spending → ₹500 crore increase in GDP
🇮🇳 India's Multiplier
India's fiscal multiplier is estimated at around 1.5–2.5 in the short run (studies by IMF, RBI). PM Kisan Samman Nidhi (₹6,000/yr to farmers) works through a relatively high MPC among poor farmers — they spend most of any incremental income → stronger multiplier effect.
💡 Keynes's Famous Quote
"In the long run, we are all dead." Keynes argued that classical economists who said "don't worry, the economy will self-correct in the long run" were being unhelpfully vague. People suffer in the short run. Government has a role to stabilise demand NOW.

IS-LM Model Advanced

The IS-LM model shows simultaneous equilibrium in two markets: the goods market (IS) and the money market (LM). Developed by Hicks (1937) interpreting Keynes.

IS Curve (Investment = Saving): Shows all combinations of output (Y) and interest rate (r) where the goods market is in equilibrium.

  • Slopes downward in Y-r space
  • Higher r → less investment (I) → lower output (Y)
  • Shifts right: ↑G, ↑I, ↑C, ↓T, ↑exports
  • Shifts left: ↓G, ↑T, ↓investment

LM Curve (Liquidity Preference = Money Supply): Shows all Y-r combinations where money market is in equilibrium.

  • Slopes upward in Y-r space
  • Higher Y → more money demand (transactions) → higher r to equilibrate
  • Shifts right: ↑ money supply (RBI OMOs, lower CRR)
  • Shifts left: ↓ money supply
IS-LM Model — Joint Equilibrium
■ IS: Goods market  |  ■ LM: Money market  |  ● Equilibrium (Y*, r*)
💡 IS-LM in Practice
When RBI cuts repo rate (↓r) → LM shifts right → equilibrium Y rises. When government increases spending (↑G) → IS shifts right → both Y and r rise. The rise in r may "crowd out" some private investment — the crowding out effect.
AD-AS Model (Aggregate Demand – Aggregate Supply) Core

The AD-AS model operates at the macroeconomic level — showing the overall price level (P) vs aggregate output (Y).

AD (Aggregate Demand): Total demand in the economy = C + I + G + NX. Slopes downward because:

  • Real balance effect: Higher P reduces real wealth → lower C
  • Interest rate effect: Higher P → higher money demand → higher r → lower I
  • Exchange rate effect: Higher P → currency appreciates → exports fall

AS (Aggregate Supply):

  • SRAS: Upward sloping. Higher prices → firms supply more (wages sticky in short run)
  • LRAS: Vertical at potential output Y*. In the long run, all prices adjust; output determined by factors, not prices.
AD-AS with SRAS & LRAS

Stagflation: When SRAS shifts left (supply shock like oil crisis) → higher P AND lower Y simultaneously. The 1970s oil shock — India and globally. Hard to treat with conventional policy (fixing inflation means contractionary policy that worsens unemployment further).

Phillips Curve Advanced

The Phillips Curve shows the empirical relationship between inflation and unemployment — discovered by A.W. Phillips (1958) studying UK data 1861–1957.

Short-run Phillips Curve: Inverse relationship — lower unemployment → higher inflation (demand is hot). Higher unemployment → lower inflation.

Long-run Phillips Curve (Friedman-Phelps, 1968): Vertical at the Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU). In the long run, there's no trade-off. Attempts to sustain unemployment below NRU will only cause accelerating inflation.

Stagflation Exception: In the 1970s, both inflation AND unemployment rose together (OPEC oil shock shifted SRAS left), breaking the short-run relationship. The Phillips Curve can shift outward.

Phillips Curve — Short Run & Long Run
■ Short-run trade-off  |  ■ Long-run vertical at NRU
💡 India's Phillips Curve
India's inflation-unemployment relationship is complicated by disguised unemployment and informal sector dominance. When headline CPI is controlled, formal unemployment may not move much. The RBI's MPC (Monetary Policy Committee) focuses more on the CPI target than on a rigid unemployment target.
Short Run, Medium Run & Long Run in Macro Core

🇮🇳 India's COVID Recovery
Short run (FY21): GDP fell 6.6% — demand collapse, sticky wages couldn't adjust fast enough. Medium run (FY22-23): Recovery as prices adjusted, demand returned, firms rehired. Long run: Depends on structural reforms — PLI schemes, infrastructure, education — to raise India's potential output (Y*).
Fiscal Policy in the IS-LM Framework Core

Fiscal Policy: Government decisions on spending (G) and taxation (T) to influence aggregate demand and output.

Expansionary Fiscal Policy
↑G or ↓T → AD rises → IS shifts right → higher Y and r. Used in recessions. Example: India's ₹20 lakh crore COVID stimulus (2020).
Contractionary Fiscal Policy
↓G or ↑T → AD falls → IS shifts left → lower Y and r. Used when inflation is high. India's fiscal consolidation path (FRBM Act targets).

Crowding Out Effect: When G rises, government needs to borrow → bond supply rises → interest rates rise → private investment (I) falls. IS shifts right, but the r rise partially offsets the output gain.

Fiscal Deficit & Debt
Fiscal Deficit = G − T (when G > T) Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments Revenue Deficit = Revenue Expenditure − Revenue Receipts

India's FY24 Fiscal Deficit Target: 5.1% of GDP India's Fiscal Deficit (FY23): 5.9% of GDP
💡 FRBM Act (Fiscal Responsibility & Budget Management Act, 2003)
Mandates the Indian government to reduce fiscal deficit to 3% of GDP (currently relaxed). Provides a rule-based framework to prevent unsustainable deficits. The "escape clause" (invoked during COVID) allows breaching the target during exceptional circumstances.

Automatic Stabilisers: Built-in fiscal mechanisms that stabilise the economy without deliberate policy action:

  • Progressive income tax: when incomes fall in recession, tax burden falls automatically → cushions the blow
  • Unemployment benefits: when unemployment rises, transfers rise automatically → sustains consumption
  • In India: MGNREGS acts as a partial automatic stabiliser in rural areas
Open Economy Fundamentals Core

An open economy trades goods, services, and assets with the rest of the world. India is an increasingly open economy (trade to GDP ratio ≈ 45%).

Balance of Payments (BoP)
BoP = Current Account + Capital Account + Financial Account + Errors & Omissions

Current Account (CA) = Trade Balance (X − M) + Net Services + Net Primary Income (remittances, profits) + Net Secondary Income (transfers)

India's CA Deficit (FY23): ~2.0% of GDP (goods deficit offset by services surplus + remittances) India is world's #1 remittance recipient (~$125 bn in 2023)
Nominal Exchange Rate
Price of one currency in terms of another. E.g., 1 USD = ₹83.5 (April 2024). Determined by demand-supply in the forex market.
Real Exchange Rate
Nominal rate adjusted for price levels. RER = e × (P* / P). Measures relative purchasing power — true competitiveness of exports.
Appreciation
Rupee buys more foreign currency (₹80 → ₹75 per USD). Good for importers (cheaper imports). Bad for exporters (their goods costlier abroad).
Depreciation
Rupee buys less foreign currency (₹80 → ₹85 per USD). Good for exporters. Bad for importers and those with dollar debt.
Marshall-Lerner Condition
Currency devaluation improves trade balance (reduces deficit) only if: |PED_exports| + |PED_imports| > 1

In the short run, condition often NOT met → J-Curve effect: After devaluation, trade balance worsens initially (contracts already in place), then improves as quantities adjust.
🇮🇳 India's J-Curve Experience
When the rupee depreciated sharply in 2013 (to ₹68/USD), India's import bill (especially oil, gold) spiked in rupee terms. Export response took ~6 months. Eventually, IT exports and textiles became more competitive. RBI intervened using forex reserves to prevent overshooting.

Absolute Advantage — Adam Smith (1776) Foundational

A country has an absolute advantage in producing a good if it can produce it using fewer resources (more efficiently) than another country.

Simple Example
Cloth (hrs/unit)Wine (hrs/unit)
India48
Portugal63
India has absolute advantage in Cloth (4 < 6). Portugal has absolute advantage in Wine (3 < 8). Smith's prescription: each country specialises in what it's absolutely better at, and trades.

Limitation: What if one country is absolutely better at everything? Should it still trade?

Comparative Advantage — David Ricardo (1817) Foundational

Comparative advantage: A country should produce and export goods in which its opportunity cost is lowest — even if it has an absolute disadvantage in all goods. This is one of the most powerful insights in economics.

Opportunity Cost Approach
Comparative advantage lies where: Opportunity Cost is LOWEST

OC of X in country A = Units of Y foregone per unit of X produced Compare across countries → produce where OC is lower
🇮🇳 India-US Example
Software (worker-days)Wheat (worker-days)
India24
USA11
USA is absolutely better at both. But: OC of Software in India = 4/2 = 2 units of wheat. OC of Software in USA = 1/1 = 1 unit of wheat. USA has lower OC in software. India's OC of Wheat = 2/4 = 0.5 units of software. USA's OC of Wheat = 1. India has comparative advantage in wheat; USA in software. Both gain from trade even though USA is absolutely better at both.

Enter worker-days (or hours) needed to produce 1 unit of each good in each country

💡 Gains from Trade
Comparative advantage shows that trade expands total global production and consumption possibilities. Both countries can consume beyond their Production Possibility Frontier (PPF). Ricardo's insight holds even when countries differ in size, technology, or wage levels — as long as relative costs differ.
Heckscher-Ohlin (H-O) Theory & Extensions Advanced

H-O Theorem (1919–33): Countries export goods that intensively use their abundant factor and import goods that intensively use their scarce factor.

  • Labour-abundant country (India) → exports labour-intensive goods (textiles, leather, software services)
  • Capital-abundant country (USA/Germany) → exports capital-intensive goods (machinery, aircraft, pharmaceuticals)

Leontief Paradox (1953): Wassily Leontief found that USA (capital-abundant) actually exported labour-intensive goods. Challenged H-O theory. Explanations: human capital (skilled labour is "capital"), technology gaps, natural resources.

Terms of Trade (ToT): Ratio of a country's export price index to import price index.

Terms of Trade
ToT = (Export Price Index / Import Price Index) × 100

ToT > 100 or improving: same exports buy more imports (favourable) ToT < 100 or deteriorating: need more exports to buy same imports (unfavourable)

India's ToT often hurt by oil price spikes (major oil importer)

🔮 Deep-dive — New Trade Theory (Krugman, 1979)
Krugman showed trade can arise even between identical countries due to: (1) Economies of scale — specialisation allows larger production → lower costs. (2) Product differentiation — consumers love variety (German vs Japanese cars). India-EU trade in similar goods (intra-industry trade) fits this better than H-O.
WTO — World Trade Organization (2026 Context) Core

The WTO is the international body that regulates global trade rules. Established in January 1995, replacing GATT. Headquartered in Geneva. As of 2026, 166 member countries including India (founding member).

Most Favoured Nation (MFN)
If you give one WTO member a trade concession (lower tariff), you must give it to all members equally. Core non-discrimination principle. Exceptions: FTAs/RTAs, preferential treatment for developing countries (GSP).
National Treatment
Once goods enter a country, they must be treated no less favourably than domestically produced goods. Prevents hidden protectionism through internal taxes/regulations.
Dispute Settlement Body (DSB)
WTO's judicial arm. Countries file complaints → panels adjudicate → Appellate Body rules (though US blocked new Appellate Body appointments 2019 onwards, causing a crisis). India has been a frequent user of DSB.
Doha Development Agenda
Launch in 2001 — promised to address developing country concerns, especially agriculture subsidies. Largely stalled. 2026: mini-packages agreed but comprehensive deal elusive.
💡 WTO 2026 — Key Issues
1. Appellate Body crisis: USA's continued block on appointments — interim Multi-party Interim Appeal Arrangement (MPIA) as workaround.
2. E-commerce negotiations: Moratorium on customs duties on digital transmissions (India & South Africa opposed it, but it held through MC13 in 2024).
3. Fisheries subsidies: First major agreement in MC12 (2022) — disciplines on subsidies contributing to overfishing. India got special provisions for small-scale fishers.
4. Investment facilitation: New plurilateral deal on investment facilitation for development, though India not a participant.
5. TRIPS waiver: COVID vaccine waiver agreed partially (2022), implementation ongoing.
🇮🇳 India at WTO — Key Positions
India consistently defends: (1) Right to food security — Public Distribution System and MSP. (2) Special and Differential Treatment (S&DT) for developing countries. (3) Right to use TRIPS flexibilities for public health. India has blocked or modified several WTO deals to protect domestic interests, earning a reputation as a tough negotiator.
GATT — General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade Core

GATT was signed in 1947 to reduce barriers to international trade. It became the de facto framework for global trade rules until the WTO replaced it in 1995. India was a founding member.

Key GATT Principles:

  • MFN: Equal treatment to all members (GATT Art. I)
  • National Treatment: No discrimination once goods enter (GATT Art. III)
  • Tariffs only: Tariffs preferred over quotas/quantitative restrictions (QRs)
  • Transparency: Publish trade laws and regulations
  • Reciprocity: Trade concessions negotiated on "something for something" basis
💡 Article XX — General Exceptions
GATT Article XX allows countries to deviate from trade rules for: (a) public morals, (b) human, animal, plant health, (g) conservation of exhaustible natural resources. India has used Art. XX defences in disputes. But must not be "arbitrary discrimination or disguised restriction on trade."

TRIPs (1994/1995) is the most comprehensive multilateral agreement on intellectual property (IP). It set minimum standards all WTO members must provide.

🇮🇳 India's Section 3(d) — Novartis Case
TRIPs allows countries to define what constitutes a patentable invention. India's Patents Act Section 3(d) prevents the granting of patents for new forms of known substances (like salts, polymorphs) unless they show significantly enhanced efficacy — specifically to prevent "evergreening" (pharma companies filing trivial modifications to extend patent life).

In the landmark Novartis vs Union of India (2013), the Supreme Court upheld Section 3(d), rejecting Novartis' patent for Gleevec (imatinib mesylate, a cancer drug). This protected India's ability to supply affordable generic cancer medicines globally.

Compulsory Licensing (CL): TRIPS Article 31 allows governments to issue a CL — allowing a third party to produce a patented product without the patent holder's consent, under specific circumstances (national emergency, non-commercial use).

India issued its first CL in 2012 for Nexavar (sorafenib tosylate, liver cancer drug) by Natco Pharma against Bayer — reducing price from ₹2.8 lakh/month to ₹8,880/month.

💡 Doha Declaration on TRIPS & Public Health (2001)
Affirmed that TRIPS should not prevent countries from protecting public health. Allowed least-developed countries (LDCs) to delay pharmaceutical patent compliance. Enabled countries to use compulsory licensing for HIV/AIDS, malaria, TB medicines. A landmark victory for developing countries.
Agreement on Agriculture (AoA) Core

The AoA (1994, part of Uruguay Round) was the first multilateral agreement to discipline agricultural trade. Three pillars:

📦
Market Access
Convert non-tariff barriers (quotas) to tariffs ("tariffication"), then reduce tariffs. Developed countries: cut 36% over 6 years. Developing (India): cut 24% over 10 years.
💰
Domestic Support
Limit trade-distorting support (AMS — Aggregate Measurement of Support). Three "boxes": Amber (reduce), Blue (constrained), Green (exempt — deemed non-trade distorting).
🚢
Export Subsidies
Reduce and eventually eliminate export subsidies (most harmful trade distortion). Developed countries faced deeper cuts. Export subsidies formally prohibited after Nairobi MC (2015).
🇮🇳 India's Agriculture Concerns
Public Stockholding for Food Security (PSH): India's FCI procures food grains at MSP and holds public stocks. Under AoA, subsidies measured against 1986-88 reference prices — highly outdated, making India's food subsidies appear far larger than they actually are.

India (with G-33 coalition) has consistently sought a permanent solution for PSH. Currently protected by a "peace clause" (Bali 2013) — no dispute can be raised even if India breaches limits — but a permanent fix remains elusive. MC13 (2024) provided no resolution.
Subsidy Boxes (AoA)
Amber Box: Directly linked to output/price → trade-distorting → REDUCE (AMS limit) Blue Box: Linked to production limits → partially distorting → allowed with conditions Green Box: Not linked to output/price → non/minimally distorting → EXEMPT

Green Box includes: R&D, food security stockholding, disaster relief, decoupled payments, environmental programmes
India argument: MSP/PDS is Green Box (food security); WTO members dispute this
Trade & Environment Advanced

The relationship between international trade and environmental sustainability is increasingly central to global trade governance.

Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) — EU, 2026 ▼

The EU's CBAM prices the carbon content of imports from non-EU countries. Sectors: steel, cement, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity, hydrogen. Full implementation from January 2026. Importers must buy CBAM certificates matching the carbon price of EU ETS.

Impact on India: India exports ~₹14,000 crore worth of goods in CBAM-covered sectors to EU annually. Indian steel, aluminium exporters face additional compliance costs. India has raised concerns at WTO (Art. XX justification vs trade barrier debate).

India's response: Developing own carbon pricing mechanism; Green Steel framework; green hydrogen mission.

WTO & Environmental Exceptions (GATT Article XX) ▼

Countries can restrict trade to protect the environment under Art. XX(b) (human/animal/plant health) and XX(g) (conservation of exhaustible natural resources). But such measures must not be "arbitrary discrimination or disguised restriction."

Key WTO cases: US-Shrimp (sea turtle protection), EC-Asbestos (carcinogens), Brazil-Retreaded Tyres (environmental protection upheld by Appellate Body).

Basel Convention & CITES ▼

Basel Convention (1989): Controls transboundary movement of hazardous wastes. Prevents rich countries dumping e-waste, toxic waste in poor countries. India ratified it. Recent "plastic waste" amendment (Basel Convention + plastic, 2021).

CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species): Controls wildlife trade. Appendix I: banned trade. Appendix II: regulated. India is a party — protects tigers, rhinos, elephants.

💡 Pollution Haven Hypothesis
Theory: strict environmental regulations in developed countries push polluting industries to developing countries with lax standards. Developing countries become "pollution havens." Empirical evidence is mixed — India has attracted some polluting FDI, but also produces clean energy equipment. Race to the bottom vs race to the top in environmental standards.

Behavioural Economics Advanced

Classical economics assumes rational agents. Behavioural economics (Kahneman, Thaler, Tversky) shows real people are predictably irrational.

Loss Aversion
Losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman-Tversky). "Pain of losing ₹100 > joy of gaining ₹100." Explains why people hold losing stocks too long.
Anchoring
First piece of information (anchor) disproportionately influences decisions. E.g., initial price shown in negotiations sets the anchor. MRP (Maximum Retail Price) in India is a deliberate anchor.
Nudge Theory
Small changes in "choice architecture" can significantly change behaviour without restricting freedom. Richard Thaler (Nobel 2017). E.g., auto-enrolment in pension schemes; opt-out vs opt-in organ donation.
Bounded Rationality
Herbert Simon: people don't maximise — they "satisfice" (find a good enough solution). Limited time, information, and cognitive capacity constrain optimal decision-making.
Present Bias
People overvalue immediate rewards vs future rewards. Explains low savings rates, addiction, procrastination. Jan Dhan Yojana tried to address this through auto-saving mechanisms.
Framing Effect
How a choice is presented changes the decision. "90% fat-free" vs "10% fat" → same thing, different perception. India's GST: 18% on non-AC restaurants vs 5% AC sounds very different but structure matters.
💡 India's Behavioural Policy
Jan Dhan Yojana (nudge: easy zero-balance accounts) dramatically expanded banking. Aarogya Setu and CoWIN during COVID used nudges. The government's "Swacch Bharat" campaign attempted a social norm nudge on sanitation behaviours.
Game Theory — Basics Advanced

Game theory analyses strategic interactions between rational agents — where each person's outcome depends on others' decisions. Foundation of modern micro, IO, and international economics.

Nash Equilibrium: A set of strategies where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, given the strategies of others. John Nash (Nobel 1994).

Prisoner's Dilemma — Classic Game
Player B: CooperatePlayer B: Defect
A: CooperateBoth get 3 yrs (Good)A: 10 yrs, B: 1 yr
A: DefectA: 1 yr, B: 10 yrsBoth get 5 yrs (Nash Eq.)
Nash Equilibrium: Both Defect → both get 5 years. But if both cooperated, they'd each get only 3. Individual rationality leads to collectively worse outcome. Applies to: OPEC production cuts, India-Pakistan nuclear race, firm advertising wars.
Dominant Strategy
A strategy that is best regardless of what the other player does. In Prisoner's Dilemma, Defect is a dominant strategy for both.
Repeated Games
When games are played repeatedly, cooperation becomes sustainable (Tit-for-Tat strategy). Explains cartels, diplomatic agreements, repeated business relationships.
Sequential Games
One player moves first, others observe and respond. Solved by Backward Induction. Stackelberg model in oligopoly.
Cournot Duopoly
Two firms choose quantities simultaneously. Nash equilibrium: each chooses best response to other's output. Total output: more than monopoly, less than perfect competition.
Public Finance — Taxation & Public Goods Core

Public goods are non-rival (one person's consumption doesn't reduce availability) and non-excludable (can't prevent someone from using it). Market fails to provide them adequately.

Pure Public Goods
Non-rival + Non-excludable. Free-rider problem → under-provision. Examples: national defence, street lighting, lighthouse, clean air.
Merit Goods
Society deems them deserving of more consumption than individuals would freely choose. Positive externality. Examples: education, healthcare, vaccines. Government subsidises or mandates them.

💡 GST in India — July 2017
Goods and Services Tax replaced 17+ central and state taxes. Dual structure: CGST + SGST (intra-state) or IGST (inter-state). 4 rate slabs: 5%, 12%, 18%, 28%. Essentials (food, medicine) mostly exempt or 0-5%. Luxury goods at 28% + cess. Aims to unify India's fragmented indirect tax system.
Money Supply & RBI's Monetary Policy Tools Core
M0 — Reserve Money
Currency in circulation + Bankers' deposits with RBI. Also called "High-powered money" or monetary base. Directly controlled by RBI.
M1 — Narrow Money
Currency + Demand deposits (current/savings accounts). Most liquid measure of money supply.
M3 — Broad Money
M1 + Time deposits (FDs). The most comprehensive measure; what RBI typically tracks for monetary policy. India's M3 growth is closely watched.
Money Multiplier
mm = M3/M0 = 1/CRR (simplified). If CRR = 4%, mm = 25. A ₹100 increase in base money → ₹2,500 increase in broad money via bank lending.

💡 India's MPC (Monetary Policy Committee)
Constituted in 2016. 6 members: 3 RBI officials (Governor has casting vote) + 3 external members appointed by government. Meets 6 times/year. Target: CPI 4% ± 2%. FY24: Held repo at 6.5% for several consecutive meetings amid disinflation path.
Development Economics Advanced

Development economics studies how low-income countries can transform their economies to achieve sustained growth, reduce poverty, and improve wellbeing.

HDI — Human Development Index
Composite of: Life expectancy (health) + Education (mean + expected years of schooling) + GNI per capita (income). India's HDI: 0.644 (Medium Human Development, rank 134/193 in 2023). Created by UNDP.
Amartya Sen's Capability Approach
Development is not about income — it's about expanding human "capabilities" (freedoms to live a life one values). Health, education, political freedom are ends in themselves, not just means. Foundation of HDI.
MPI — Multidimensional Poverty Index
UNDP + Oxford measure. 10 indicators across health, education, living standards. India's MPI headcount fell significantly 2015-2021 (415 million lifted out of MPI poverty, UNDP 2023).
Structural Transformation
Lewis model: shift of labour from low-productivity agriculture to high-productivity manufacturing/services. India's challenge: services-led growth without manufacturing transformation (unlike China's path).
💡 India's Development Paradox
India grew rapidly (6-8% GDP) but its social indicators lagged — called the "India paradox." High GDP growth didn't automatically translate to HDI gains. Schemes like PM-KISAN, Ayushman Bharat, Jal Jeevan Mission, PM Awas Yojana aim to directly address human development gaps.
Growth Theory — Solow & Beyond Deep-dive

Growth theory asks: what determines long-run economic growth? Why are some countries rich and others poor?

Solow Growth Model (1956): Output depends on capital (K), labour (L), and technology (A). Key insights:

Solow Production Function
Y = A · F(K, L) (A = Total Factor Productivity / technology)
In per capita terms: y = A · f(k) (y = Y/L, k = K/L)

Steady State: Investment = Depreciation s · f(k*) = (n + δ) · k* (s = savings rate, n = population growth, δ = depreciation)

Convergence: Poor countries grow faster than rich countries (diminishing returns to capital)
  • Diminishing returns to capital: Each additional unit of capital adds less output → growth slows → steady state
  • Technology (A): Only technological progress drives long-run growth per capita
  • Convergence hypothesis: Poor countries should catch up to rich ones (conditional convergence — empirically partial)

Endogenous Growth Theory (Romer, 1986; Lucas, 1988): Argues growth can be sustained through knowledge accumulation, human capital, R&D — which have increasing returns (not diminishing). Policy can permanently affect growth rates.

🇮🇳 India's Growth Drivers
Demographic dividend: large, young working-age population (till ~2040) → if employed productively, major growth driver. TFP growth: infrastructure investment, ease of doing business, digital transformation (India Stack, UPI). Structural transformation: moving workers from agriculture (low productivity) to manufacturing and services.
International Finance — IMF, World Bank & SDRs Advanced
IMF — International Monetary Fund
Provides financial assistance to countries facing BoP crises. Conducts Article IV consultations. Sets conditions for loans (structural adjustment). India borrowed from IMF in 1966, 1981, 1991. India is now a net creditor to IMF.
World Bank Group
IBRD: loans to middle-income countries. IDA: concessional loans to poorest countries. India still borrows from IDA and IBRD for development projects (health, infrastructure).
SDR — Special Drawing Rights
IMF's reserve asset — a basket of currencies (USD, EUR, CNY, JPY, GBP). Not a currency itself. Used for settlements between central banks. India's SDR allocation (2021): ~$17.86 bn as part of COVID-era $650 bn SDR allocation.
Currency Crisis
Sudden depreciation due to speculative attacks or BoP stress. India's 1991 crisis: BoP crisis → IMF loan → LPG reforms. More recently: Lira crisis (Turkey), Rupee stress (2013 "taper tantrum", 2022).
💡 India 1991 — The Turning Point
India's forex reserves fell to less than 2 weeks of import cover. India had to pledge gold to the Bank of England to get emergency loans. The crisis led to the New Economic Policy 1991 — LPG reforms (Liberalisation, Privatisation, Globalisation) under PM PV Narasimha Rao and FM Manmohan Singh. GDP grew from ~$320 bn (1991) to ~$3.5 trillion (2024).
